Policy making is influenced by a number of factors, including electoral politics, ideological biases of actors involved in the policy making process, and the interlocks between corporate and government entities. This influence is also exercised by shaping public opinion through mass media. We study this influence through the two effects of mass media, namely agenda-setting and framing. The ability of mass media to influence the importance placed on various public topics is called agenda-setting. The phenomenon of media focusing attention on certain events and then placing them within a field of meaning or perception is called framing.

In this respect, we study four of the most prominent economic policies covered by mass media, namely Demonetization, Aadhaar, GST, and Farmers’ Protest. We select these economic policies for our analysis as they are among some of the main policy events that have happened over the last few years.

To study agenda-setting, we look at the coverage of different aspects in each of these policies in mass media and in the social media community of followers of mass media houses. To study framing, we define five constituencies which are the dominant frames of perception corresponding to these events. These constituencies are poor, who typically benefit from wealth distribution strategies; middle class, consumers who have disposable income, and benefit through tax breaks and lower prices; corporate, which include big corporates, formalization, economic growth, free-market policies, and minimum governance; informal sector, driven by small enterprises and aided by slow formalization of industries and trade, also including agriculture; and government in terms of pro/anti viewpoints towards the state. For each policy event, we map each aspect to these five constituencies based on whether the aspect supports or opposes or is not applicable to the particular constituency. For example, for Demonetization, the aspect on [Queues at banks and ATMs] is classified as pro-middle class because most articles on this aspect were negatively writing about the problems caused to the common people in getting cash at ATMs. The same aspect is classified as anti-government because negative articles on this aspect were critical of the government’s apathy and lack of foresightedness in handling the issue.

Alignment of Newspaper's towards different constituencies



As stated earlier, we map each aspect corresponding to a policy to five constituencies or frames of presentation. Each aspect is thus provided an alignment score of -1/0/+1 corresponding to each constituency based on if the majority articles in the aspect speak positively or negatively about the constituency. Using the (aspect,constituency) alignment matrices for the four events, we obtain a constituency vector (of five dimensions) for each news-source. Briefly speaking, each of these scores in the vector is the aggregated sentiment of the news-source towards the constituency, weighted by the aggregate coverage of the constituency. We then perform a principal component analysis or PCA of the (newspaper,constituency) matrix to obtain the plot shown here. The first component (x-axis) here denotes a pro-poor alignment towards the right, and a pro-corporate and pro-informal sector alignment towards the left. The second component (y-axis) shows and pro-government and pro-middle class alignment towards the top. Using the plot on the right, we try to answer the question: "Do news-sources have a bias towards or against constituencies like the poor, middle class, corporate, informal sector, and government?".

We find that Hindu, Deccan Herald, and IE are centrist news-sources, with a slight alignment towards the poor. In other words, these papers cover issues of the poor more than the others. Hindustan Times is seen to be biased towards political issues or issues related to the government. Times of India is seen to cover issues of the middle class. Finally, Telegraph is seen to be biased towards giving more coverage to the informal sector and small trades.

It is important to note that here we consider an article’s alignment to a constituency based on the face-value of what the newspaper states about the constituency. For example, if the newspaper states that a particular policy is helpful for the poor, we assume the article to be aligned to the poor constituency. We do not impose any ideological evaluation on the views expressed in the media, of whether the statements are true or not from different perspectives.

  • Aadhaar

  • An initiative by the government to give every Indian resident a biometric-based unique identification number. The data is collected by the Unique Identification Authority of India (UIDAI), a statutory authority under the jurisdiction of the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology. The issue has been criticized owing to lack of security and privacy in citizens’ data collection and storage mechanisms, and also because of an allegedly faulty implementation of the platform that led to denial of welfare benefits to many poor people.
  • Demonestisation

  • A policy event where the government on 8 November, 2016 banned all INR 500 and INR 1000 banknotes with the motive of curtailing the use of illicit and counterfeit cash used to fund illegal activity and terrorism. The move was widely criticized owing to multiple problems caused to common people due to sudden depletion of liquidity, irregularities in norms of exchanging old currency notes, cash exhaustion in ATMs, and so on.

  • Farmer's Protest

  • A series of protests by farmers in India including the ones at Madhya Pradesh (Mandsaur protest) and Maharashtra (Kisan long march) demanding better prices for production of crops, loan waivers, and forest rights, among others. The issue is highly active politically with a lot of involvement of different politicians and political parties.

  • GST

  • An indirect tax levied in India on the sale of goods and services. It is levied at each step of the production value-chain with an effort towards formalization in the industry and simplification of multiple types of taxes which preceded the GST regime. Since its implementation there have been intense debates through on its complexity and problems in implementation which have impacted the overall growth of the economy.
