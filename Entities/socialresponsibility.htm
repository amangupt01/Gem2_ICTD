Corporate Social Responsibility

In 2013, the government introduced a regulation mandating companies with profits of over Rs 5 Crore to donate 2% of their profits towards Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) in areas like healthcare, education, rural development, environment, national relief, gender equality, heritage & culture, cleanliness, slum area development, sports and armed forces welfare etc. We were curious to know if CSR investments made in particular geographies could have been used as a means by corporate entities to build favourable networks with politicians affiliated with these geographies. The research paper on "What Drives Location Preference for Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) Investments in India?" can be seen here.
Data sources: Data about CSR investments made during the years 2014-15, 2015-16 and 2016-17 was obtained from www.csr.gov.in.

The Logic behind CSR Investments

In the table, the leftmost column shows the factors that might influence CSR spending, and on the right, we have six regression models. While the predictive power of the models is low, many of the factors have a statistically significant relationship with determining CSR funding.

(a) In the first regression model, the two significant variables (or factors) observed were “whether the constituency in which a CSR investment was made is GEN and not backward” and “whether the constituency is SC and not backward”, with p-value less than 0.001, for a positive relationship with the dependent variable. In other words, CSR spending is more in constituencies that are of general category and non-backward, or are of SC category and non-backwards.It cannot be stated with statistical significance whether backward constituencies are able to draw CSR investments, other than ST constituencies that show a positive relationship with p-value less than 0.01.

(b) In the second model, the “Vote Margin percentage between the current MP elected from a constituency and the runners up candidate from the constituency in the 2014 Lok Sabha elections” was added as an independent variable. This variable is also found to have a significant positive coefficient and small p-value. This shows that if the vote difference percentage is higher, the CSR amount spent in the corresponding constituency is more than the amount spent in other constituencies.

(c) In the third model, the aim was to find out if the alignment of the current MP from the constituency with the ruling party of the union or state government has any relation with the CSR amount spent in the MP’s constituency. Result of this analysis shows that the state alignment variable is significant with a positive relationship. In other words, the CSR amount spent in the constituencies of MPs belonging to the ruling party of the state is more, compared to other constituencies. No relationship was found between the alignment of MPs with the ruling party at the centre.

(d) In the fourth model, we wanted to see if the MP from a constituency being a Minister in the Union Government may have an influence on the CSR expenditure. Therefore, an independent variable, “whether the MP is a central minister” was added. We find that this variable is not significantly related with CSR spending.

It is however observed that a number of ministers are from the Rajya Sabha. We also verify if the CSR amount spent in a district has any relation with the district selected by the Rajya Sabha MP (also a union minister) for spending his/her MPLAD fund. Results show that it has a strong positive relation with a p-value 0.005. This means that if any district in a constituency is selected by the Rajya Sabha MP for using the MPLAD fund, the CSR spending in that district is higher compared to the others.

(e) In the fifth model, the vote margin percentage was added to the four independent variables used in the fourth model. It was found that vote margin remains a significant feature and there is a slight increase in R squared value of the model. In other words, the vote-margin, state alignment, and MPLAD funding are all robust relationships in their own right.

(f) In the sixth model, we tried to observe if the variable, “whether there is any overlap between the industry sector of the company involved in CSR and the sectors of influence of the MP’s ministry (if MP from that constituency is a minister)” has any impact on the CSR spending. This could indicate for example, that by contributing CSR funding to the constituency of a minister, the company could be buying favours from the ministry with which the minister is associated and which is of particular interest to the company as well. This variable was found to be very significant with a positive coefficient, which implies that if the MP is a minister and the policies framed by his/her ministry influences the sector that the company works in, then the CSR amount spent in the constituency is higher.

In 2014-15, Maharashtra and Tamilnadu were the two most preferred states for CSR expenditure. Whereas, Lakshadweep, Andaman and Nicobar, Mizoram, Nagaland, and Tripura were the states where companies were least interested to spend their CSR fund in. Also, Approx 5% of the total was spent in 4 most backward states of India ( Jharkhand, Odisha, Bihar and Uttar Pradesh). Similar trends are observed even when considering per capita investments.

Sector wise CSR expenditure

During 2014-15 which was the first financial year when CSR was made mandatory, sectors covering Education, Differently Abled people, and Livelihood were the most preferred sectors (Rs 3188.09 Cr i.e. approx. 32%), followed by Health, Eradicating Hunger, Poverty And Malnutrition, Safe Drinking Water, and Sanitation (Rs 2525.92 Cr i.e. 25%). These trends have remained consistent in FYs 2015-16 & 2016-17 as well.

CSR Expenditure of Top Ten companies

Analysis of expenditure by Top 10 companies shows that none of these companies contributed towards Clean Ganga Fund and Swachh Bharat Kosh. Sectors like Education, Differently Abled, Livelihood and Health, Eradicating Hunger, Poverty and Malnutrition, Safe Drinking Water, Sanitation were subscribed by the majority of the companies. Expenditure by PSUs or other Govt owned establishments is not diversified, whereas Pvt companies spend their money on various activities under a variety of sectors.
